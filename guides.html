🎙️ Podcasting Guides

Practical, no-fluff guides for creators at every stage — from first episode to first sponsor.

1. How to start a podcast from zero

Pick one clear topic and one clear listener

"A show about business" fails; "a show helping African farmers sell better" wins. If you can name your ideal listener, you have a show.

Gear: start smaller than you think

A phone in a quiet room beats an expensive mic in an echoing one. Record in a carpeted room or even a parked car. Upgrade to a USB microphone (£50–£80) only after episode 10 — most podcasts quit before episode 8, so prove your consistency first.

Structure every episode the same way

Intro (who you are, what today's episode gives the listener) → main content in 2–3 segments → recap → one call to action. Listeners love rhythm.

Publish weekly, same day, no exceptions

Consistency beats quality in the first year. A good-enough episode every Tuesday builds more audience than a perfect episode "whenever."

2. How to grow past your first 100 listeners

Your title is 80% of the click

Episode titles should promise a result, not describe a recording: "How I cut my feed costs by 30%" beats "Episode 14 — chat with John."

Turn one episode into ten pieces of content

Every episode contains quotes, clips, and lessons. Post them on the platforms where your listeners already are. One hour of recording should feed your social media for a week. Be a guest before you seek guests

Appearing on 5 shows in your niche grows you faster than 50 social posts. Their audience already listens to podcasts — your perfect recruit.

Ask for the follow, every episode

Listeners forget. One sentence — "if this helped, follow the show so you don't miss next week" — measurably grows retention.

3. How to make money from your show

The money ladder, in order

1) Listener support — even 20 true fans paying a small monthly amount is real revenue. 2) Affiliate income — recommend tools you genuinely use, earn a commission. 3) Your own product or service — the highest margin: consulting, a course, your farm's produce, anything you sell. 4) Sponsorships — viable from roughly 500+ downloads per episode in a clear niche.

Niche beats numbers

A show with 300 loyal poultry farmers is more sponsorable than a show with 3,000 random listeners. Sponsors buy audiences, not download counts.

Price with confidence

A simple starting rule for niche shows: £20–£25 per 1,000 downloads for a 60-second mid-roll ad — and more if your niche is valuable (finance, agriculture, B2B).

4. How to sound professional (without a studio)

Room first, mic second

Soft surfaces kill echo: curtains, blankets, cushions, wardrobes. The cheapest "studio" in the world is recording under a blanket — broadcasters genuinely do it.

Distance and level

Stay a fist's width from the microphone, and speak across it rather than straight into it to avoid popping sounds.

Edit less than you think

Remove long silences, coughs and false starts — then stop. Over-edited shows sound robotic. Your natural voice is the brand.

Level your loudness

Aim for consistent volume across the whole episode and between episodes (the industry standard is -16 LUFS for stereo podcasts — most editors have a one-click "normalize" for this).

5. How to get your show everywhere (distribution)

Your RSS feed is your show's passport

Every platform — Apple, Spotify, YouTube Music and beyond — reads the same RSS feed. Submit it once to each directory; new episodes then appear everywhere automatically.

Submit to the big five minimum

Apple Podcasts, Spotify, YouTube Music, Amazon Music and Pocket Casts cover the vast majority of listeners worldwide.

Don't ignore the open web

An embedded player on your own website turns visitors into listeners and belongs to you forever — no platform algorithm in the way. (You can try CastAmplify's free embed player on the homepage.)

Think beyond English early
